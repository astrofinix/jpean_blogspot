The Philippines is at its Dark Point of No Return


We all want long-lasting and moving change. Filipinos have long aspired for change that can transform their lives to greater heights yet the force to do so is on an undisrupted cycle, even worsening by the day. Listing these three fundamental forces in play is key in understanding why a hopeful future will neither be a swift nor easy task, especially to the generation that will precede in power in governing Philippines.
01. Its Laws: The Political System is Broken.

A country's constitution, in this case the 1987 constitution, is like a blueprint in a country. It is the most basic block of a country's laws, and policies for several if not years, but decades of its lifespan. It also, when written or designed poorly, can perpetuate a giant stump in a country's progress.

Several flaws of the constitution that have been tested by time are: having no enacting law that ends political dynasties, unclear wording on how people's initiative can enact change themselves, the lack of definition on how the constitution can be amended or improved, and the overpower of the supreme court among others. Explaining further the first four challenges mentioned, these problems are a result of poorly defined powers in the constitution. For example, although, the constitution prohibits political dynasties:
The State shall guarantee equal access to opportunities for public service, and prohibit political dynasties as may be defined by law.
~Article 2. Section 26. 1987 Constitution.
There is no clearly defined law that enforces it. And perhaps one of the reasons why no such law exists is because 73 out of 80 provinces in the Philippines, there are political dynasties that clash for power.

Another example of the constitution's lack of definition is how the People can vote in making change themselves. Although framed in the constitution, there is no law clearly explaining how it can be done.

Amendments to this Constitution may likewise be directly proposed by the people through initiative upon a petition… …The Congress shall provide for the implementation of the exercise of this right.
~Article 18, Section 2. 1987 Constitution.
Moving on, the power of congress to amend the constitution is vaguely worded as well. Although it states that it needs three fourths of the congress to propose change, it stops there without defining how the vote is done either at both the house of representatives and the senate (two parts of the law-making government) or separately. Thus, for years of proposing amendments after another, there is yet to have change in the 1987 constitutions.

Lastly, but not the last of the problems embedded in the constitution, the power of the Supreme Court makes it unbalanced and overpowered. First there are no limits in the length of service a judge can serve, and second it’s not as independent nor co-equal to the other branches. It can struck down laws, and even the voting to do so can be influenced by whose president appointed them.

This major flaw is hinted at by the striking down of the “Roe v. Wade” case in the United States of America (USA), where it struck down a decade long protection in a safe access of abortion because of its conservative body the composes the Supreme Court. Now, with the ruling, if the logic is also borrowed to other cases, such as same-sex marriage, access to contraception, and even interracial marrigage, these protections can be struck down as well. But how does this relate to the Philippines? Well, the constitution, especially the supreme court, is almost a replica from the USA’s version, which means that landmark rulings by the Supreme Court can be dissolved and can have long-lasting impact on the People.
02. Its Government: It’s a Popularity Contest.

The Philippines is governed by three branches, from the President and the ministries with them, the Congress, and the Supreme Court from which it intends to co-regulate each other.

But, now, with the rise of populism, the government is composed by, if not by political dynasties, popular people (celebrities, influences, etc.). Moreover, the appointment of judges in the supreme courts and secretaries in departments are those favored by the President, instead of people with the right expertise and intent to serve. As for the recent years, the last administration of Duterte has squashed the opposition that is supposed to create a healthy discourse in making, and enacting laws. Now, it is starting unravel itself the unregulated power of laws that suppress people’s rights (anti-terror law) and stomp progressive laws in being enforced (divorce, abortion, same-sex union, etc.).

All in all, even at the appointment of willing people in creating change, it can feel as going in the grain of the system. As mentioned before, it is almost impossible to amend the constitution, and that locks out laws that can protect people’s rights.
Marriage is a special contract of permanent union between a man and a woman
~Chapter 1. Article 1. Family Code of the Philippines.
For example, the legalization of divorce has been in the bin of the Congress, and yet still not one has passed yet due to the protection of marriage deeply embedded in the constitution. In addition, the Supreme Court has repeatedly overruled and even dissolved laws, for example the abortion bill passed by 2011 was strucked down, and in 2019, any attempts for a same sex union is overrulled to be unconstitional, all because of interpretation made from the constitution which prohibits these enactments.

[The State] shall equally protect the life of the mother and the life of the unborn from conception.
~Article 2. Section 12. 1987 Constitution.
03. Its People: A Curse of its Own.

The Government is, in no doubt, plagued with a lot of corruption and abuses from the power vested in them, but the reason these people remain in power is because of the very people that elect them. From its deprived populace of education, the long curse and an almost never-ending cycle of power remains; further fueled by rampant disinformation. We have seen an unprecedented deprivation of people’s welfare, from accessible healthcare, security of rights for all, a labor law that does not rely on overexploitation, conservation of wildlife and environment among others. All of which puts us behind from the world, only longing for change that actually empowers us.
Epilogue

Now, you have a glimpse of the Political Life in the Philippines, although at a glance it is somewhat invisible, it is subtle yet powerful enough to take a dark point of no return for all of us.

With the question, how can this change? In my view, there are two possible solutions for you. Either (1) migrate to another country that guarantees one’s rights and really empowers its people. Although it's not a privilege for everyone, it is a well-endowed right to exit a Republic. (2) Be a disruption in this cycle, a revolution, not of violence, but a catalyst of change. Somehow, it is the seemingly way of ever getting out of this cycle. Breaking the old constitution, to reform it to a 21st Century vision of the Philippines. But this does not happen in a day, and requires a fully awakened spirit of the people. Know that we do not have to be severely and extremely depressed into such conditions just to realize we need change.
TL; DR (Too Long; Didn't Read.)

The Philippines is verging into a deep recession that is subtle enough to pass the eyes of the majority. From its unamendable laws, a populist government, and a slumbered People, all playing part in this never-ending cycle. All of it boils down to getting out of it either from moving out of this country or going into a long road of progress that is down to revolt against the current system.
